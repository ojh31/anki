What does the mnemonic <b>SAMPLE</b> stand for in Wilderness First Aid, and what is it used for?	<b>SAMPLE</b> is a patient history mnemonic used during a secondary assessment:<br><br><b>S</b> — Signs and Symptoms<br><b>A</b> — Allergies<br><b>M</b> — Medications<br><b>P</b> — Pertinent past medical history<br><b>L</b> — Last oral intake (food/water)<br><b>E</b> — Events leading up to the incident<br><br>It helps you gather the information needed to make an evacuation decision and guide ongoing care.
What does the mnemonic <b>AVPU</b> stand for, and when do you use it?	<b>AVPU</b> is a rapid scale for assessing mental status / level of responsiveness, used during the <b>Initial Assessment</b> (nervous-system corner) and for ongoing monitoring:<br><br><b>A</b> — Alert / Awake (oriented to person/place/time/event)<br><b>V</b> — responds to <b>Verbal</b> stimulus (talking, their name)<br><b>P</b> — responds to <b>Painful</b> stimulus only (e.g. pinch, sternal rub)<br><b>U</b> — Unresponsive / Unconscious (no response to verbal or painful stimulus)<br><br>A decline in AVPU is a red flag for a serious problem (e.g. head injury, shock, hypothermia) and warrants rapid evacuation.
What does the <b>STOPEATS</b> mnemonic stand for, and when do you use it?	Use STOPEATS when there's <b>no obvious trauma</b> — it prompts you to consider a <b>medical or environmental</b> cause:<br><br><b>S</b> — <b>Sugars</b> (hypo- / hyperglycemia)<br><b>T</b> — <b>Toxins</b> (poisons, food, animal vectors, alcohol, medications)<br><b>O</b> — <b>Oxygen</b> (hypoxia)<br><b>P</b> — <b>Pressure</b> (increased intracranial pressure)<br><b>E</b> — <b>Electricity</b> (seizure, electrical currents)<br><b>A</b> — <b>Altitude</b> (HAPE, HACE, nitrogen narcosis)<br><b>T</b> — <b>Temperature</b> (hypo- / hyperthermia)<br><b>S</b> — <b>Salts</b> (hyponatremia)
What are the three phases of the NOLS <b>Patient Assessment System (PAS)</b>?	<b>1. Scene Size-up</b><br><b>2. Initial Assessment</b> — aka <b>"Stop &amp; Fix"</b> (life threats)<br><b>3. Focused Exam &amp; History</b>
In the NOLS PAS, what do you assess during the <b>Scene Size-up</b>?	• <b>Scene safety</b> (hazards, BSI / PPE)<br>• <b>MOI / NOI</b> — mechanism of injury or nature of illness<br>• <b>Number of patients</b> and resources needed<br><br>Done <i>before</i> approaching the patient.
What is the goal of the <b>Initial Assessment</b> (aka <b>Stop &amp; Fix</b>) in the NOLS PAS?	Find and treat immediate <b>life threats</b> — problems with <b>airway, breathing, or circulation</b> that will kill the patient within minutes if not corrected.<br><br>The name <i>Stop &amp; Fix</i> captures it: you pause everything else and fix life threats before moving on.
What does <b>ABCDE</b> stand for in the Initial Assessment?	<b>A</b> — Airway (with C-spine precautions as indicated)<br><b>B</b> — Breathing<br><b>C</b> — Circulation (pulse, perfusion, serious bleeding)<br><b>D</b> — Disability (level of responsiveness via AVPU, spine)<br><b>E</b> — Environment / Expose (protect from cold/heat; expose injuries as needed)
What three components make up the <b>Focused Exam &amp; History</b> (third phase of the NOLS PAS)?	<b>1. Physical exam</b> — head-to-toe, focused on findings<br><b>2. Vital signs</b> — baseline plus trends over time<br><b>3. SAMPLE history</b>
What does <b>MOI</b> stand for, and what does it describe?	<b>Mechanism of Injury</b> — the manner in which an <i>external physical force</i> caused an injury on the body.<br><br>Identified during the <b>Scene Size-up</b>; helps you detect likely <b>serious underlying injuries</b> (e.g. spinal, internal) before they're visible.
Which mechanisms of injury (MOIs) <b>transfer significant force</b> and should raise suspicion of serious underlying injury?	• Fall from <b>twice the patient's body height</b><br>• <b>Tumbling fall</b> — body rotates more than once<br>• <b>Compression or distraction</b> of the head &amp; neck<br>• <b>Blunt force</b> impact<br>• <b>Penetrations</b> by sharp objects<br>• <b>Rapid deceleration</b> into a stopped object (e.g. skier vs. tree)
What does <b>NOI</b> stand for, and what does it describe?	<b>Nature of Illness</b> — a medical condition you suspect may be affecting the patient due to <b>illness or environmental factors</b> (non-trauma).<br><br>Identified during the <b>Scene Size-up</b> to frame the initial impression for medical patients.
What general-impression clues suggest a <b>Nature of Illness (NOI)</b>?	• <b>Difficulty moving</b><br>• <b>Altered mental status</b><br>• <b>Difficulty breathing</b> — e.g. tripod posturing<br>• <b>Pain</b> with an unknown source<br>• <b>Abnormal appearance</b> — skin signs, grimace, odd speech<br>• Patient or peers say <i>"something's not right"</i>
What are the <b>three critical body systems</b> that must work together for survival, and what happens when one fails?	<b>1. Nervous</b> · <b>2. Respiratory</b> · <b>3. Circulatory</b><br><br>When any one of these systems fails, <b>the process of dying begins</b>.
What are the key <b>parts and function</b> of the <b>nervous system</b>?	<b>Parts:</b> brain &amp; spinal cord<br><b>Function:</b> automatically tells the <b>lungs to breathe</b> and the <b>heart to pump</b>.
What are the key <b>parts and function</b> of the <b>respiratory system</b>?	<b>Parts:</b><br>• Upper airway — nose &amp; mouth<br>• Lower airway — lungs &amp; alveoli<br><br><b>Function: oxygenation.</b> We breathe through the upper airway; oxygen is absorbed into the bloodstream via the lower airway (alveoli).
What are the key <b>parts and function</b> of the <b>circulatory system</b>?	<b>Parts (a closed-loop pump system):</b><br>• <b>Heart</b> — the pump<br>• <b>Blood vessels</b> — the pipes<br>• <b>Blood</b> — the fluid<br><br><b>Function: perfusion</b> — blood carries oxygenated blood throughout the body to vital organs (brain, lungs, other tissues). A problem with <i>any</i> of the three parts leads to <b>Shock</b>.
What are the three <b>underlying causes of death</b>, and which body system does each correspond to?	• <b>Circulation</b> → <b>Shock</b><br>• <b>Respiration</b> → <b>Respiratory Failure</b><br>• <b>Nervous</b> → <b>Brain Failure</b>
What is <b>Shock</b>?	<b>Inadequate systemic perfusion</b> — the circulatory system fails to deliver oxygenated blood throughout the body.
What is <b>Respiratory Failure</b>?	<b>Inadequate oxygenation</b> — the respiratory system fails to get enough oxygen into the bloodstream.
What is <b>Brain Failure</b>?	<b>Inadequate oxygenation &amp; perfusion to a vital organ</b> (the brain).
What are the <b>three types of shock</b>, and which part of the circulatory system fails in each?	Each type corresponds to a failure in one part of the closed-loop pump system:<br><br>• <b>Fluid</b> (blood) fails → <b>Volume Shock</b><br>• <b>Pump</b> (heart) fails → <b>Cardiogenic Shock</b><br>• <b>Pipes</b> (blood vessels) fail → <b>Vascular Shock</b>
What is <b>Volume Shock</b>, and what causes it?	<b>Fluid loss</b> from the circulatory system.<br><br><b>Causes:</b> external or internal bleeding, vomiting, diarrhea, sweating, dehydration, burns.
What is <b>Cardiogenic Shock</b>, and what causes it?	<b>Failure of the heart to adequately pump oxygenated blood.</b><br><br><b>Causes:</b> heart attack or heart failure from clogged arteries, or chest trauma.
What is <b>Vascular Shock</b>, and what causes it?	<b>Abnormal dilation of the blood vessels</b> — loss of the ability to regulate vessel tone.<br><br><b>Causes:</b> spinal injury, anaphylaxis, poisoning, drug use, septic infection.
What's the difference between <b>compensated</b> and <b>decompensated</b> shock?	<b>Compensated:</b> the body maintains perfusion by raising pulse and respiratory rate.<br><br><b>Decompensated</b> (aka "the crash"): the body can no longer compensate. <b>Life-threatening</b> — if the underlying cause isn't treated, the patient will die.
What is the <b>vital sign pattern</b> for Volume Shock?	As fluid volume decreases:<br><br>• <b>Pulse</b> — rises, until the body can no longer compensate<br>• <b>Respiration</b> — rate rises; breathing becomes <b>shallow and labored</b><br>• <b>Skin</b> — becomes <b>pale</b> due to the <b>shell/core effect</b><br>• <b>Mental status</b> — normal at first, then a steady decline toward unconscious
What is the <b>shell/core effect</b>?	As shock progresses, blood <b>shifts away from the skin (shell)</b> toward <b>vital organs (core)</b>. Makes the patient's skin look <b>pale, cool, clammy</b>.
How do the vital signs in <b>Vascular Shock</b> differ from Volume Shock?	Pulse and respiration still rise as the body tries to compensate — but because the vessels have lost the ability to regulate dilation/constriction, <b>there is no shell/core effect</b>. Skin may instead look <b>flushed and warm</b> as blood pools in peripheral vessels.
How do the vital signs in <b>Cardiogenic Shock</b> differ from Volume Shock?	The "pump" (heart) is the problem, so the pulse can be <b>rapid, slow, or irregular</b>. Otherwise vital signs are similar to Volume Shock.
What is the <b>general treatment</b> for any patient in shock?	Treat the underlying cause as effectively as you can:<br><br>• <b>Stop fluid loss</b><br>• Give <b>sips of fluids</b> to rehydrate<br>• <b>Prevent heat loss</b> by insulating the patient<br>• Provide <b>supplemental oxygen</b><br>• <b>Monitor vital signs</b> and note changes<br>• <b>Evacuate quickly!</b><br><br><i>Early recognition is key to survival.</i>
What is the <b>type-specific treatment</b> for <b>Volume Shock</b>?	• <b>Direct pressure</b> to stop bleeding<br>• <b>Carefully rehydrate</b> the patient
What is the <b>type-specific treatment</b> for <b>Cardiogenic Shock</b>?	• Begin <b>CPR and assisted breathing</b> as needed<br>• Aid the patient in taking any <b>life-saving medications</b> — e.g. <b>Aspirin</b>, <b>Nitroglycerin</b>
What is the <b>type-specific treatment</b> for <b>Vascular Shock</b>?	• Treat the underlying <b>infection, poisoning, or illness</b><br>• Treat <b>anaphylaxis</b> with <b>Epinephrine</b> if protocols allow
What is an <b>artery</b>?	A blood vessel that carries <b>oxygenated blood away from the heart</b>.
What is a <b>vein</b>?	A blood vessel that <b>returns oxygen-poor blood back to the heart and lungs</b>.
What is a <b>capillary</b>?	A <b>small vessel</b> that delivers blood from arteries to the body's cells.
What are the main functions of the three <b>blood components</b>?	• <b>Red blood cells</b> — carry oxygen<br>• <b>White blood cells</b> — fight infection<br>• <b>Platelets</b> — clot blood
How do you recognise an <b>arterial bleed</b>, and why is it the most serious?	<b>Appearance:</b> bright red, spurting<br><b>Severity:</b> <b>most serious</b> — difficult to control due to the high pressure from the heart's contractions.
How do you recognise a <b>venous bleed</b>?	<b>Appearance:</b> dark red, <b>oozing and flowing steadily</b>.
How do you recognise a <b>capillary bleed</b>, and how serious is it?	<b>Appearance:</b> slow, leaking.<br><b>Severity:</b> not serious. Classic example: a nosebleed.
What is the general sequence for <b>controlling a critical bleed</b>?	<b>1.</b> Don <b>PPE</b><br><b>2.</b> Quickly <b>expose and inspect</b> the wound; identify the source of bleeding<br><b>3.</b> Apply <b>well-aimed direct pressure</b> on the point of bleeding<br><b>4.</b> If bleeding is controlled → maintain it with a <b>pressure bandage</b><br><b>5.</b> If bleeding <i>cannot</i> be controlled with direct pressure → apply a <b>tourniquet</b>
What <b>PPE</b> should you wear when controlling a critical bleed?	• <b>Two pairs of gloves</b> — the extra layer gives you a clean pair if the outer is soaked or tears<br>• <b>Protective eyewear</b>
How do you apply <b>direct pressure</b> to a critical bleed, and what can you improvise with if you have no dressing?	Apply <b>well-aimed direct pressure</b> on the point of bleeding with your <b>fingers or the palm of your hand</b>.<br><br>If no pad or dressing is available, use <b>any clean fabric or cloth</b>, or a <b>gloved hand alone</b>.
Once direct pressure has controlled a bleed, how do you <b>maintain pressure</b>?	Apply a <b>pressure bandage</b>: place layers of gauze over the wound, then wrap an Ace bandage <b>tightly</b> over it to create continuous direct pressure.
When do you apply a <b>tourniquet</b>, and where do you place it?	<b>When:</b> bleeding <i>cannot</i> be controlled with direct pressure.<br><br><b>Placement:</b> <b>2 inches above the wound</b>, <b>proximal to the heart</b>.<br><br><b>Never</b> place a tourniquet over the <b>knee, elbow, or neck</b>.
Once a tourniquet is in place, what three things do you do?	<b>1.</b> <b>Monitor</b> the wound — ensure bleeding remains controlled<br><b>2.</b> Keep the tourniquet <b>visible</b> for future responders<br><b>3.</b> <b>Note the time</b> applied on the patient
How do you <b>improvise a tourniquet</b> if you have no commercial one?	Use a <b>bandage</b> plus an <b>unbreakable torsion device</b> — e.g. a tent pole, a sharpie, or several sticks taped together. Secure firmly and note the time applied.<br><br><b>Why "unbreakable":</b> if the torsion device breaks, the tourniquet releases and the built-up pressure makes <i>more</i> blood escape than before.
What clues suggest <b>internal bleeding</b>?	Internal bleeding is <b>difficult to detect</b> — look for:<br><br>• A <b>significant or high-impact MOI</b><br>• <b>Tenderness</b> on the abdomen during the physical exam (palpate the <b>4 quadrants</b> and note findings)<br>• <b>Signs and symptoms of shock</b><br>• <b>Traumatic injury</b> to the <b>abdomen, torso, pelvis, or femur</b>
What is <b>guarding</b>?	A protective response to abdominal pain: the patient <b>flexes their abdomen</b> and/or <b>hunches forward</b>. A clue to suspect <b>internal injury or bleeding</b>.
What pertinent questions should you ask when you suspect <b>internal bleeding</b>?	• Is the patient <b>guarding</b>?<br>• Is there any <b>bloody output</b>?<br>• Have they had <b>abdominal pain</b> for a prolonged time (<b>&gt;24 hrs</b>)?<br>• Is the patient <b>pregnant</b>?
What is the <b>treatment (Tx)</b> for suspected internal bleeding?	• <b>Treat for shock</b><br>• Keep the patient <b>warm and calm</b><br>• Provide <b>supplemental oxygen</b><br>• <b>Monitor vital sign</b> changes<br>• <b>Evacuate immediately!</b>
What is <b>respiration</b>?	<b>Gas exchange</b> between the lungs and the bloodstream, taking place during <b>inspiration</b> (breathing in) and <b>expiration</b> (breathing out). Oxygen enters the blood via the <b>alveoli</b>; carbon dioxide leaves.
What are the two main categories of <b>difficulty breathing</b>, and how do you tell them apart by sound?	<b>Upper Airway Obstruction</b> — noisy breathing on <b>inspiration</b><br><b>Lower Airway Constriction</b> — noisy breathing on <b>expiration</b><br><br>The phase of the breath that sounds wrong localises the problem.
What causes an <b>Upper Airway Obstruction</b>, and what are the S/S?	<b>Causes:</b> choking; swollen tongue, throat, or esophagus.<br><br><b>S/S:</b> wheezing, drooling, noisy breathing on <b>inspiration</b>.
What causes <b>Lower Airway Constriction</b>, and what are the S/S?	<b>Causes:</b> swelling of the mucous membranes and/or accumulation of pus in the lungs — <b>asthma</b>, <b>bronchitis</b>, <b>anaphylaxis</b>.<br><br><b>S/S:</b> tightness in the chest; noisy breathing on <b>expiration</b>; fever.
What is the <b>progression</b> of untreated difficulty breathing?	<b>Respiratory Distress → Respiratory Failure → Respiratory Arrest</b><br><br>If not fixed, difficulty breathing will march through these stages to death.
What are the S/S of <b>Respiratory Distress</b>?	• Patient speaks in <b>short, choppy sentences</b><br>• Patient is <b>anxious</b><br>• <b>A</b> on the AVPU scale
What are the S/S of <b>Respiratory Failure</b>?	• Patient can only speak <b>1–2 words at a time</b><br>• Patient is <b>lethargic</b><br>• <b>A</b>, <b>V</b>, or <b>P</b> on the AVPU scale
What are the S/S of <b>Respiratory Arrest</b>?	• Patient is <b>no longer breathing</b><br>• <b>U</b> on the AVPU scale
What is the <b>treatment</b> for difficulty breathing?	• <b>Position of Comfort</b> — sit the patient in a position that allows them to breathe deeply<br>• <b>Reassurance</b> — keep the patient calm and comfortable<br>• <b>Oxygen</b> — remind the patient to take <b>long, deep, effective breaths</b><br>• <b>Positive Pressure Ventilation (PPV)</b> — assist breathing with a barrier device (e.g. pocket mask) if necessary<br><br>Use the <b>Head Tilt-Chin Lift</b> to open the airway if it is kinked or blocked.
What is <b>Positive Pressure Ventilation (PPV)</b>, and when do you use it?	Assisting the patient's breathing by <b>pushing air into their lungs</b> using a <b>barrier device</b> (e.g. a <b>pocket mask</b>).<br><br>Used when the patient's own breathing is <b>inadequate</b>.
What happens <b>initially</b> when tissue or an organ is irritated or injured?	The tissue <b>leaks and/or swells</b>. The resulting <b>build-up or loss of fluid creates pressure</b>, and that pressure is what causes the problems (e.g. swelling and bruising at any injury site).
What is the <b>three-stage progression</b> of untreated tissue damage caused by swelling/fluid build-up?	<b>Ischemia → Infarction → Necrosis</b><br><br>Fluid build-up and swelling drive the progression.
What is <b>ischemia</b>?	The <b>initial blockage</b> of blood flow to an area due to fluid or swelling.
What is <b>infarction</b>?	<b>Inadequate local perfusion</b> — blood and oxygen can no longer reach the affected tissue.
What is <b>necrosis</b>?	<b>Tissue death</b> due to continued infarction.
What is the time window before infarction becomes necrosis in a <b>limb</b>?	Approximately <b>2 hours</b>.
What is the time window before infarction becomes necrosis in the <b>brain</b>?	Only <b>5–6 minutes</b> — orders of magnitude shorter than a limb, which is why airway and circulation are the top priorities in the Initial Assessment.
Obstruction to tissue can lead to infection. Give an example of a <b>local</b> vs. <b>systemic</b> infection.	<b>Local infection:</b> pimple<br><b>Systemic infection:</b> sepsis
The <b>Initial Assessment</b> is a triangle with a corner for each critical body system. How many things do you assess at each corner?	<b>Two things per corner</b> — one pair for each of the three critical systems (circulatory, respiratory, nervous). Six items total.
In the Initial Assessment, what <b>two things</b> do you assess for the <b>circulatory system</b>, and what does each trigger?	• <b>Pulse</b> — if absent, begin <b>CPR</b><br>• <b>Critical bleeding</b> — control with <b>direct firm pressure</b>
In the Initial Assessment, what <b>two things</b> do you assess for the <b>respiratory system</b>, and what does each trigger?	• <b>Airway</b> — open using the <b>head-tilt, chin-lift</b><br>• <b>Breathing</b> — if inadequate, aid the patient with ventilations via a <b>pocket mask</b>
In the Initial Assessment, what <b>two things</b> do you assess for the <b>nervous system</b>?	• <b>AVPU</b> — mental status scale<br>• <b>Spinal precautions</b> — hold <b>C-spine</b> if significant MOI or any head/neck/spine pain
How does the <b>brain</b> send and receive messages throughout the body?	Via the <b>spinal cord</b>, which runs through the bony <b>vertebrae</b>. <b>Spinal cord roots</b> exit through openings in the vertebrae and travel as <b>nerves</b> that reach the rest of the body.
Why is a spinal cord injury so dangerous, and why are broken vertebrae especially concerning?	Even <b>small cuts to the spinal cord</b> can cause <b>permanent disability, paralysis, or death</b>.<br><br>A broken vertebra is a direct threat because <b>sharp bone edges</b> can cut into the spinal cord if the patient moves.
How many <b>vertebrae</b> are in each region of the spine?	• <b>Cervical</b> — 7<br>• <b>Thoracic</b> — 12<br>• <b>Lumbar</b> — 5<br>• <b>Sacral</b> — 5
What happens to the <b>sacral vertebrae</b> over time?	They begin to <b>fuse</b> anywhere from puberty into adulthood.
Which <b>mechanisms of injury</b> should make you suspect a <b>spinal injury</b> specifically?	MOIs involving significant force to the head, neck, or spine:<br><br>• Falls from heights <b>twice body height</b><br>• <b>Compression / axial loading</b> — landing on the head or tailbone<br>• Excessive <b>flexion</b> — chin forced to chest<br>• Excessive <b>extension or rotation</b> — e.g. diving, tumbling falls<br>• <b>Distraction</b> of the head — e.g. attempted hanging<br>• <b>Penetration</b> — stabbing or impalement near the spine<br>• <b>Sudden deceleration</b> or impact to the body
What are the <b>S/S of a spinal injury</b>?	• <b>Pain and tenderness</b> along the spine<br>• <b>Loss of CSM</b> in any of the 4 extremities<br>• <b>Numbness, tingling, or burning</b> sensations anywhere in the body
What is the <b>treatment</b> for a suspected spinal injury?	• <b>Immediately hold C-spine</b> and encourage the patient not to move<br>• If needed, <b>roll the patient onto their back</b><br>• <b>Call EMS</b> and await professional help if possible
If you must <b>move a patient with a suspected spinal injury</b> (for environmental, respiratory/circulatory, or safety reasons), how do you do it?	Move with the <b>spine in line</b> — keep the head, neck, and back aligned in a neutral position. Move the patient <b>as little as possible</b> while keeping the spine stable.
What is a <b>Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)</b>?	Any <b>change in mental status</b> following head trauma — e.g. <b>loss of consciousness (LOC)</b> or lacking full orientation (<b>A&amp;Ox4</b>).
What is <b>Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP)</b>, and why does it happen?	A more serious form of TBI: the brain is swelling but the <b>rigid skull gives it nowhere to expand</b>, so pressure builds up inside the cranium.<br><br>Any irritated tissue swells or leaks fluid — with the brain, that swelling has no room, which makes the injury life-threatening.
What are the <b>early S/S</b> of a TBI?	• <b>Altered mental status</b><br>• <b>Headache</b> and <b>nausea</b><br>• Any <b>deformity of the skull</b><br>• <b>Disoriented, irritable, confused, or combative</b> behavior
What are the <b>late-stage S/S</b> of a TBI?	• <b>Loss of coordination</b> and/or <b>speech</b><br>• <b>Visual disturbances</b> — pupils <b>unequal</b><br>• <b>Fluid or blood</b> coming from <b>ears, eyes, or nose</b><br>• <b>Bruising</b> — <b>raccoon eyes</b> (around eyes) or <b>Battle's sign</b> (behind ears / around temples)
What two specific signs during a progressing TBI indicate <b>ICP</b> — a life-threatening condition?	• A <b>continually increasing headache</b><br>• <b>Persistent vomiting</b> — <b>3× or more</b>
What pertinent <b>SAMPLE history</b> questions should you ask a patient with a suspected TBI?	• Did the patient <b>lose consciousness</b>?<br>• Was the patient <b>wearing a helmet</b>?<br>• Has the patient had a <b>recent TBI</b>? When? How serious?
What is <b>Second Impact Syndrome (SIS)</b>?	When a person suffers <b>multiple TBIs in a short period of time</b> — classic risk groups include boxers, football players, and extreme athletes.<br><br>SIS <b>can be fatal</b>.
What is the <b>treatment</b> for a suspected TBI or ICP?	• Suspect a possible <b>spinal injury</b> and <b>hold C-spine</b><br>• <b>Do NOT stop</b> the flow of fluid from the nose, mouth, or ears<br>• Consider the <b>airway</b> — be prepared to <b>suction</b> if vomit is present<br>• If the patient is vomiting, <b>secure them safely on their side</b><br>• <b>P.R.O.P.</b><br>• <b>Continually monitor and reassess</b><br>• <b>Evacuate quickly</b> if S/S of ICP develop
What is a <b>stroke</b>?	A <b>"brain attack"</b> caused by <b>clogged or ruptured blood vessels</b> in the brain.
What is the <b>STR</b> mnemonic for recognising a stroke?	<b>S — Smile:</b> have the patient smile. Is one side drooping?<br><b>T — Talk:</b> notice if the patient has slurred or difficult speech<br><b>R — Raise:</b> ask the patient to raise both arms. Are both sides moving equally?
What is the <b>treatment</b> for a suspected stroke?	• <b>Early recognition</b> using <b>STR</b><br>• <b>Do NOT give blood thinners!</b><br>• <b>Evacuate immediately</b>
When should you apply <b>C-spine precautions</b>?	Immediately, if the patient has:<br>• a <b>significant MOI</b>, <b>or</b><br>• <b>any pain</b> to the head, neck, or spine
What does it mean to <b>hold C-spine</b>?	Manually hold the patient's <b>head and neck in alignment with their back</b>, keeping the spine in a neutral line to protect the spinal cord from further injury.
How do you perform the <b>physical exam</b> in the Focused Exam &amp; History?	A <b>complete head-to-toe</b> examination <b>with physical contact</b> — don't just look, you have to <i>touch</i>.
During the head-to-toe physical exam, if you find a problem, where else should you check and why?	Look to the <b>skin</b> over the area for <b>bruising or bleeding</b> — signs of capillary leak or deeper underlying injury.
What four findings do you <b>look and feel for</b> during the head-to-toe physical exam?	<b>D</b>eformities<br><b>O</b>bstructions<br><b>T</b>enderness<br><b>S</b>welling<br><br>(Mnemonic: <b>DOTS</b>.)
What does <b>CSM</b> stand for (checked during the physical exam)?	<b>C</b>irculation · <b>S</b>ensation · <b>M</b>otion (aka Motor)
Where do you check <b>CSM</b> during the physical exam?	In each of the <b>four extremities</b> (both hands and both feet) — screens for neurovascular compromise distal to any potential injury.
What are the two <b>long bones</b> you're most likely to be asked about, and where are they?	• <b>Femur</b> — upper leg<br>• <b>Humerus</b> — upper arm
What is a <b>ligament</b>?	Connective tissue that connects <b>bone to bone</b>.
What is a <b>tendon</b>?	Connective tissue that connects <b>muscle to bone</b>.
What is <b>cartilage</b>?	Connective tissue that is <b>softer than bone</b>; aids in <b>mobility</b> and <b>shock absorption</b>.
What is a <b>fracture</b>?	Any <b>discontinuity in bone tissue</b>.
What is a <b>dislocation</b>?	A <b>misalignment of bone at a joint</b>.
What is a <b>sprain</b>?	A <b>stretched or torn ligament</b>.
What is a <b>strain</b>?	A <b>stretched or torn tendon</b>.
How do you <b>assess a suspected musculoskeletal injury</b>?	• <b>Look</b> — remove clothing and observe the injury site; note any bruising, deformity, or abrasions<br>• <b>Ask</b> — MOI, pain level, range of motion, crepitus<br>• <b>Feel</b> — for deformity and swelling; compare to the <b>uninjured side</b><br>• <b>Check CSM</b> — circulation, sensation, motion
When considering <b>evacuation</b> for a musculoskeletal injury, what three questions guide the decision?	• Is the injury <b>stable vs. unstable</b>?<br>• Is it severe and <b>affecting a joint or long bone necessary to walk</b>?<br>• Is there <b>CSM compromise</b>?
What does the <b>HIRICE</b> mnemonic stand for, and when is it used?	General Tx for stable and unstable musculoskeletal injuries:<br><br>• <b>H</b> — Hydrate<br>• <b>I</b> — Ibuprofen<br>• <b>R</b> — Rest<br>• <b>I</b> — Ice<br>• <b>C</b> — Compression<br>• <b>E</b> — Elevation
How do you <b>immobilize a stable injury</b>?	• After <b>20 min</b> of HIRICE, remove ice<br>• Wrap with an <b>ACE bandage</b> or other material — not so tight that it cuts off blood flow<br>• <b>Always wrap toward the heart</b><br>• Use a <b>sling/swathe</b> for upper-extremity injuries<br>• <b>Recheck CSM</b> after applying the bandage<br>• Give the patient something (stick, pole) for support while walking<br>• Evacuate carefully — don't worsen the injury
How do you <b>immobilize an unstable injury</b>?	<b>1.</b> Check <b>CSM</b><br><b>2.</b> <b>Manually stabilize</b> — have the patient hold their own stabilization if possible<br><b>3.</b> Prepare splinting materials<br><b>4.</b> <b>Measure splint</b> to the uninjured side<br><b>5.</b> <b>T.I.P.</b> (Traction Into Position)<br><b>6.</b> Apply the splint and secure it to the body — the splint must stabilize <b>above and below</b> the injury<br><b>7.</b> <b>Recheck CSM</b>
What is <b>T.I.P.</b>, and when should you NOT use it?	<b>Traction Into Position</b> — applying gentle pull to bring a displaced long bone back toward anatomical alignment before splinting.<br><br><b>Do NOT T.I.P.</b> if a <b>joint is involved</b>.<br><br><b>Stop if:</b> you encounter <b>crepitus</b> or <b>gross resistance</b>, or if <b>pain increases</b>.
What are the key <b>principles of splinting</b>?	• No single splint works for every person or limb — <b>splinting is a creative process</b><br>• <b>Restrict motion without cutting off blood flow</b><br>• The splint must stabilize <b>above and below</b> the injury<br>• Always check <b>CSM before and after</b> applying the splint<br>• Build in a way to <b>re-assess</b> the injury and splint during evacuation, to avoid causing <b>ischemia → infarction → necrosis</b>
How do you manage an <b>open fracture</b>?	<b>Evacuate!</b><br><br>• <b>Control any bleeding</b><br>• <b>Clean the exposed wound</b> carefully and thoroughly<br>• If you can't fully clean it, consider <b>leaving the injury exposed</b><br>• If evacuation is <b>&gt;4–6 hr</b>: <b>T.I.P. and splint</b><br><br><b>Tradeoff:</b> pulling bone ends back in <i>increases infection risk</i> but <i>increases survival of the bone tissue</i> (by restoring perfusion to the displaced segment).
What is the <b>epidermis</b>, and what does it do?	The <b>outermost layer</b> of skin.<br><br><b>Contains:</b> dead skin cells and bacteria<br><b>Function:</b> <b>protection from microbes</b>
What is the <b>dermis</b>, and what does it do?	The layer of skin <b>beneath the epidermis</b>.<br><br><b>Contains:</b> blood vessels, glands, hair, nerves<br><b>Function:</b> <b>thermoregulation</b>
What is the role of the <b>fat layer</b> of the skin?	<b>Energy storage</b> and <b>insulation</b>.
What is <b>fascia</b>, and what does it do?	A layer of <b>connective tissue</b> that <b>covers muscle, bones, organs, and joints</b>.
What four <b>functions of the skin</b> are compromised by a wound?	• <b>Protection</b><br>• <b>Sensation</b><br>• <b>Thermoregulation</b><br>• <b>Fluid retention</b>
What are the <b>six types of wounds</b>?	• <b>Lacerations</b><br>• <b>Abrasions</b><br>• <b>Avulsions</b><br>• <b>Amputations</b><br>• <b>Evisceration</b><br>• <b>Burns</b>
How do you distinguish a <b>high-risk</b> from a <b>low-risk</b> wound?	<b>High-risk:</b><br>• Penetrates the <b>fascia</b><br>• <b>Heavily contaminated</b><br>• <b>Open fractures</b><br>• <b>Bites, punctures, or impalement</b><br>• Burns to the <b>face, genitals, or large areas</b><br>• Involves the <b>face, eyes, or mouth</b><br>• Needs <b>aggressive field management</b><br>• <b>Difficult evacuation</b> or travel<br><br><b>Low-risk:</b><br>• Involves only the <b>dermis</b><br>• <b>Small / clean avulsions</b><br>• <b>Small lacerations</b><br>• <b>Blisters / chafing</b><br>• <b>Small-area burns</b><br>• No functional, cosmetic, or life risk<br>• <b>Manageable in the field</b><br>• <b>Easy evacuation/travel</b>
What are the three steps of <b>low-risk wound care</b>?	<b>1.</b> Wear appropriate <b>PPE</b><br><b>2. Clean</b> — high-pressure irrigation with <b>clean water or iodine solution</b><br><b>3. Cover</b> — don't seal in bacteria; extend gauze about <b>1 inch past the wound</b> on all sides<br><br><b>Prevent infection:</b> clean thoroughly. <b>Do it right the first time!</b>
How do you manage a <b>high-risk wound</b>?	• <b>Direct pressure</b> to stop the bleed<br>• <b>High-pressure irrigation</b> to remove debris<br>• <b>Do NOT close the wound</b> — closing can trap debris and cause infection<br>• Apply <b>moist gauze</b> saturated with <b>saline and/or antibiotic ointment</b><br>• Cover the moist dressing with <b>dry dressings</b> and bandage in place<br>• <b>Splint</b> the extremity to reduce movement<br>• <b>Anticipate shock</b> from blood loss and/or infection
What is an <b>avulsion</b>, and how do you treat it?	<b>Definition:</b> the <b>incomplete severing</b> of a body part — a flap of tissue remains attached.<br><br><b>Tx:</b><br>• Control bleeding with <b>direct pressure</b><br>• <b>P.R.O.P.</b> and treat for shock if necessary<br>• Once bleeding is controlled, <b>clean and bandage</b> the wound with the skin flap <b>in place</b>
What is an <b>amputation</b>, and how do you treat it?	<b>Definition:</b> the <b>complete severing</b> of a body part.<br><br><b>Tx:</b><br>• Control bleeding with <b>direct pressure</b><br>• Once bleeding is controlled, <b>clean and bandage</b> the wound<br>• <b>P.R.O.P.</b> and treat for shock if necessary<br>• Locate and preserve the severed part
How do you care for a <b>severed body part</b> after an amputation?	• Wrap it in <b>saline-soaked sterile cloth</b><br>• Place it in a <b>sealed waterproof container</b><br>• <b>Keep cool:</b> place the sealed bag inside <i>another</i> bag of <b>ice water</b><br>• <b>Never</b> place the severed part <b>directly on ice or snow</b> (freezing injury kills the tissue)
What is an <b>evisceration</b>, and how do you treat it?	<b>Definition:</b> an abdominal injury in which <b>organs protrude out</b> of the wound. The exposed organs may still be <b>functioning</b> — handle very carefully.<br><br><b>Tx:</b><br>• <b>Do NOT</b> push organs back into the body<br>• <b>Do NOT</b> apply direct pressure<br>• Cover organs with a <b>thick, moist dressing</b> or saline-soaked abdominal pad<br>• <b>Evacuate immediately!</b>
What is <b>insulin</b>, and what does it do?	A <b>hormone</b> secreted by the <b>pancreas</b> that helps <b>glucose transfer into cells</b>, where it is stored and converted into energy.
What is a <b>diabetic emergency</b>, and what's the underlying physiology?	An <b>imbalance between glucose and insulin</b>.<br><br>A <b>diabetic</b> is unable to produce an adequate amount of insulin — so the body's cells don't receive enough energy from sugar.
How do <b>hypoglycemia</b> and <b>hyperglycemia</b> compare?	<b>Hypoglycemia</b> — low blood sugar; progresses <b>rapidly</b> and can become <b>life-threatening</b>.<br><br><b>Hyperglycemia</b> — high blood sugar; progresses <b>slowly</b> over a long period.
In a wilderness setting, why do we <b>"assume hypoglycemia"</b>, and what's the key rule?	We burn more fuel in the wilderness, using up sugar faster — so we're much closer to <b>low</b> blood sugar than high.<br><br><b>Hypoglycemia kills faster</b> than hyperglycemia — withholding sugar from a hypoglycemic patient can mean <b>permanent brain damage or death</b>.<br><br><b>Always give sugar when in doubt.</b>
What are the <b>causes of hypoglycemia</b>?	• <b>Too much insulin</b><br>• <b>Not eating enough</b> food or sugars<br>• <b>Overexertion</b><br>• <b>Pancreas failure</b>
What are the <b>S/S of hypoglycemia</b>?	• <b>Slurred speech</b>, inability to communicate<br>• <b>Altered mental status</b><br>• <b>Trembling, shaking</b>, loss of coordination<br>• <b>Weakness, fainting</b><br>• <b>Pale, cool, sweaty</b> skin<br>• Progression: <b>seizures</b> → <b>U</b> on AVPU
If you're unsure whether a patient is having a <b>diabetic emergency</b>, what <b>history clues</b> can you look for?	• <b>Medical alert bracelet</b><br>• <b>Insulin kit</b><br>• Ask <b>friends or family</b>
How do you treat a <b>conscious hypoglycemic</b> patient who can swallow without difficulty?	• Provide <b>oral glucose</b><br>• If not available, give <b>easy-to-absorb sugars</b> — orange juice, candy, fruit<br>• <b>Artificial sweeteners will NOT help</b>
How do you treat a hypoglycemic patient who is <b>unresponsive, unable to follow commands, or has difficulty swallowing</b>?	• Place the patient in the <b>recovery position</b> (on their side)<br>• <b>Protect and maintain the airway</b><br>• <b>Repeatedly place small amounts of oral glucose or sugar</b> under the tongue or between gums and teeth<br>• Be very careful to <b>prevent aspiration</b><br>• <b>Evacuate immediately!</b> Life-threatening condition.
What are the <b>four routes</b> by which toxins can enter the body?	Toxins can be:<br>• <b>Ingested</b><br>• <b>Inhaled</b><br>• <b>Absorbed</b><br>• <b>Injected</b>
For <b>any toxin exposure</b>, what four general areas should you consider?	• <b>Patient factors</b> — age (older &amp; younger are more susceptible); SAMPLE for co-existing conditions and medications<br>• <b>Evacuation</b> — distance to care; does the hospital have <b>antivenin</b>? Terrain / energy cost<br>• <b>Toxic overload</b> — how much of the toxin? how long was the patient exposed?<br>• <b>Identification</b> — identify or <b>photograph the vector/substance</b>
What is the <b>National Poison Control Hotline</b> number, and what should you tell them?	<b>1-800-222-1222</b><br><br>Clearly <b>identify the substance</b> and provide details about the incident.
What are common sources of <b>ingested toxins</b>?	• Overdosed <b>medications</b><br>• <b>Personal care and cleaning products</b><br>• <b>Fuels</b><br>• <b>Plants and mushrooms</b><br>• <b>Spoiled food</b>
What is the <b>treatment</b> for ingested toxins?	• Identify the substance and <b>contact Poison Control</b><br>• Consider <b>activated charcoal</b> if available<br>• <b>Do NOT give water</b> unless advised by Poison Control<br>• <b>P.R.O.P.</b><br>• <b>Evacuate</b>
What are the <b>S/S of plant or mushroom poisoning</b>, and what is the critical timing detail?	<b>S/S:</b><br>• Nausea and vomiting<br>• Diarrhea<br>• Abdominal pain<br>• Headache<br>• Hallucinations<br><br><b>Timing:</b> symptoms may appear <b>up to 12 hours after ingestion</b>. Can progress to <b>kidney/liver failure</b> and death.
What are common sources of <b>inhaled toxins</b> in a wilderness setting?	• <b>Smoke</b> from burning poison oak or other caustic substances<br>• <b>Carbon monoxide</b> — e.g. from cooking in an unventilated snow cave or shelter<br>• <b>Chlorine gas</b> — e.g. from mixing bleach and vinegar
What is the <b>treatment</b> for inhaled toxins?	• Get the patient to <b>fresh air immediately</b><br>• <b>Anticipate respiratory distress</b><br>• <b>P.R.O.P.</b><br>• <b>Contact Poison Control</b><br>• <b>Evacuate</b> if necessary
What is the <b>generalized treatment</b> for an absorbed toxin, and when is it high-risk?	• <b>Immediately clean</b> the affected area<br>• <b>Soaps or vinegar</b> may be needed to remove oily substances<br>• <b>Leave blisters intact</b><br>• <b>Contact Poison Control</b><br><br><b>High risk</b> if <b>&gt;10% of body surface area</b> is affected:<br>• <b>P.R.O.P.</b><br>• <b>Evacuate</b>
What is the <b>mechanism, S/S, and Tx</b> of <b>poison oak</b> exposure?	<b>Mechanism:</b> <b>urushiol oil</b> — present in <i>all</i> parts of the plant, not just the leaves.<br>Mantra: <b>"Leaves of three, let it be."</b><br><br><b>S/S:</b> irritated, red, itchy, inflamed skin. Can spread to others or to new body parts via scratching/touching.<br><br><b>Tx:</b><br>• <b>Irrigate immediately</b><br>• Wash with <b>soapy water</b>, directing runoff <i>away from</i> non-affected body parts<br>• Treat with <b>calamine lotion</b> or a specialized wash (e.g. <b>Tecnu</b>)
What is the <b>mechanism, S/S, and Tx</b> of <b>stinging nettle</b> exposure?	<b>Mechanism:</b> <b>histamines</b> plus tiny <b>spikes</b> that irritate the skin.<br><br><b>S/S:</b> itching, burning, rash, small blisters. Lasts <b>hours to days</b>.<br><br><b>Tx:</b><br>• <b>Wash</b> the exposed area immediately<br>• Apply a <b>cool compress</b> to alleviate pain
What are common sources of <b>injected toxins</b>?	• <b>Needle sticks</b><br>• <b>Insects and arachnids</b> (vectors)<br>• <b>Marine animals</b>
What is the <b>generalized treatment</b> for injected toxins?	<b>Prevention first:</b><br>• <b>Know your environment</b> — research vectors before traveling<br>• Prepare with proper clothing, medications, and a plan<br><br><b>If exposed:</b><br>• <b>Identify</b> the vector — take a picture<br>• <b>Contact Poison Control</b><br>• <b>Monitor vital signs</b>; watch for developing <b>fever</b><br>• <b>P.R.O.P.</b><br>• <b>Evacuate</b> if necessary
What are the <b>S/S and Tx</b> of a <b>snake bite</b>, and what must you NOT do?	<b>S/S:</b><br>• <b>Tissue swelling</b> at the injection site<br>• <b>Paralysis, cramping, numbness</b> if neurotoxin<br><br><b>Tx:</b><br>• <b>Contact Poison Control</b><br>• <b>Splint</b> the extremity if possible<br>• <b>P.R.O.P.</b><br>• <b>Evacuate</b> to a hospital with <b>antivenin</b><br><br><b>DO NOT:</b> attempt <b>suction</b>, apply a <b>tourniquet</b>, or use <b>ice</b>.
How do you manage <b>tick exposure</b>?	<b>Check every night</b> for ticks — especially in <b>dark, moist areas</b> of the body and <b>in hair</b>.<br><br><b>Tx:</b><br>• If found, remove <b>completely</b> with <b>tweezers</b><br>• If a <b>red mark, bullseye rash, or fever</b> develops → <b>Evacuate</b>
What is an <b>allergic reaction</b>, and how do allergens enter the body?	The body's response to an <b>allergen</b> or foreign substance — the immune system releases <b>histamine</b> and other substances to confront and remove it.<br><br><b>Routes of exposure</b> (same as toxins): <b>ingested, inhaled, injected, absorbed</b>.
What are common <b>allergens</b>?	• <b>Foods and medications</b><br>• <b>Animal dander</b><br>• <b>Insect venom</b><br>• <b>Plants and pollen</b><br>• <b>Latex</b> and other materials
What are the <b>S/S of a minor to moderate allergic reaction</b>?	• <b>Local reaction</b> — swelling and tenderness at the exposure site<br>• <b>Flushed, itchy skin</b><br>• <b>Watery eyes</b>; irritated, <b>stuffy nose</b>
What is the <b>treatment</b> for a minor to moderate allergic reaction?	• <b>Oral antihistamine</b><br>&nbsp;&nbsp;— Generic: <b>Diphenhydramine</b><br>&nbsp;&nbsp;— Brand: <b>Benadryl</b>, <b>Claritin</b><br>• <b>Ice or cool</b> the affected area<br>• <b>Anti-itch ointment</b>
What is <b>anaphylaxis</b>?	A <b>severe, life-threatening allergic reaction</b> — the body responds to an allergen with an excessive response.
What are the <b>S/S of anaphylaxis</b>?	• <b>Difficulty breathing</b> — constriction of the airway<br>• <b>Swelling</b> of the face, eyelids, lips, tongue, and soft tissue of the throat<br>• <b>Systemic hives</b>, particularly on the face and chest<br>• <b>Nausea</b> and abdominal cramping<br>• A <b>drop in blood pressure</b> due to dilation of the blood vessels
How do you administer an <b>epinephrine auto-injector</b> in an anaphylaxis emergency?	• Dose: <b>0.3 mg</b><br>• Inject into the <b>middle, lateral side</b> of the patient's thigh<br>• Remove the injector and <b>gently rub</b> the injection site
What is the full <b>Tx sequence for anaphylaxis</b> (Wilderness Protocol)?	• <b>Epinephrine auto-injector</b> — 0.3 mg, mid-lateral thigh<br>• If the patient can swallow, give an <b>oral antihistamine</b> (Diphenhydramine)<br>• <b>P.R.O.P.</b><br>• <b>Second dose</b> of epinephrine after <b>10 minutes</b> if patient doesn't respond<br>• <b>Evacuate immediately</b> — even if the patient appears to recover
Why must you <b>evacuate an anaphylaxis patient</b> even if they appear to recover?	The patient may <b>relapse</b>, and the condition can become <b>fatal if more doses of epinephrine are unavailable</b>. Evacuation ensures access to further doses and definitive medical care.
What is <b>hypoxia</b>?	A <b>deficiency in the amount of oxygen reaching the tissues</b>.<br><br>Can be a symptom of many different problems causing difficulty breathing.
What is <b>asthma</b>?	A condition in which certain <b>substances, exertion, or environmental factors</b> trigger <b>inflammation of the airways</b>, causing difficulty breathing.
What is an <b>inhaler</b>?	A <b>portable device</b> used by asthmatics to deliver a <b>measured amount of inhaled medicine</b> from a pressurized canister.
What are the <b>S/S of an asthma attack</b>?	• <b>Wheezing</b> and <b>coughing</b><br>• A feeling of <b>tightness in the chest</b><br>• <b>Shortness of breath (S.O.B.)</b>
What is the <b>treatment for an asthma attack</b>?	• Have the patient <b>relax</b> and <b>stop vigorous activity</b><br>• <b>Assist the patient with their inhaler</b>
What S/S indicate <b>severe asthma</b> (Wilderness Protocol)?	• <b>Persistent altered mental status</b><br>• <b>Incomplete response</b> to treatment via inhaler<br>• Patient's condition is <b>worsening</b>, progressing toward <b>respiratory arrest</b>
What is the <b>treatment for severe asthma</b> not controllable with an inhaler?	• <b>P.R.O.P.</b><br>• <b>Epinephrine auto-injector 0.3 mg</b> — via lateral thigh<br>• <b>Rescue inhaler</b> if available<br>• <b>Immediate evacuation</b>
What are the <b>6 mechanisms</b> by which lightning can injure a person?	• <b>Direct strike</b> — lightning directly hits the person<br>• <b>Splash current</b> — lightning jumps from its target to a person<br>• <b>Ground current</b> — the charge radiates out from the strike point to a person<br>• <b>Long conductors</b> — someone touches a fence or other conductor that is hit<br>• <b>Blast injury</b> — person thrown by the exploding air caused by lightning<br>• <b>Penetrating injury</b> — person injured by debris
What are the <b>S/S of a lightning injury</b>?	• <b>Altered mental status</b> — TBI, LOC, paralysis, seizures<br>• <b>Blindness</b> or <b>deafness</b> — permanent or temporary<br>• <b>Burns</b> — superficial or internal<br>• <b>Blunt and penetrating trauma</b>
What is the <b>treatment</b> for a <b>responsive</b> patient after a lightning strike?	• <b>Treat any visible burns</b><br>• <b>Anticipate airway, cardiac, and neurological complications</b><br>• <b>Evacuate</b>
What is the <b>treatment</b> for an <b>unresponsive</b> patient after a lightning strike?	• <b>Immediately begin CPR</b> with compressions<br>• <b>Early AED defibrillation may save a life</b> in this situation
What are the <b>scene-safety priorities</b> when responding to a lightning strike?	• <b>Continually ensure the scene is safe</b> — lightning may strike twice in the same place<br>• <b>Leave water</b> and <b>put on shoes</b> if on wet ground<br>• Move to a <b>lower, covered</b> area<br>• <b>Smaller strands of trees</b> are safer than large exposed ones<br>• <b>Caves</b> may conduct electricity — <i>not</i> a recommended shelter
How should you <b>position yourself</b> if caught in a lightning storm, and what should a group do?	<b>Individual:</b><br>• Create a <b>barrier between you and the ground</b> — something rubber or non-conductive (e.g. a sleeping mat)<br>• <b>Crouch down</b> and <b>cover your ears</b><br><br><b>Group plan:</b><br>• <b>Separate</b> and <b>number off</b> — so you can account for everyone after the storm
What causes <b>altitude illnesses</b>, and how does the body try to acclimatize?	<b>Cause:</b> declining <b>oxygen content</b> in the air and decreasing <b>barometric pressure</b> cause <b>hypoxia</b>.<br><br><b>Acclimatization:</b> the body changes the <b>pH of the blood</b>, and <b>HR ↑</b> and <b>RR ↑</b>.
At what altitude can <b>Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS)</b> occur, and what factors affect susceptibility?	AMS can occur as low as <b>8,000 ft</b>.<br><br>Susceptibility varies with:<br>• <b>Age</b> — young and old are more affected<br>• <b>Physical fitness</b><br>• <b>Medical history</b><br>• <b>Genetic predisposition</b><br>• Consumption of <b>alcohol or drugs</b>
What are the <b>S/S of AMS</b>?	• <b>Headache</b><br>• <b>Loss of appetite</b><br>• <b>Nausea, vomiting</b><br>• <b>Insomnia</b><br>• <b>Weariness, exhaustion</b>
What is the <b>Tx for AMS</b>?	• <b>Descend</b> and <b>acclimatize</b> until symptoms resolve<br>• <b>Supplemental oxygen</b><br><br>If not addressed, AMS can progress to the life-threatening conditions <b>HAPE</b> and <b>HACE</b>.
What is <b>HAPE</b>, and what is the mechanism?	<b>High Altitude Pulmonary Edema</b> — the heart is unable to provide adequate blood flow, so fluid <b>backs up and leaks from capillaries of the alveoli</b> into the air spaces of the lungs.<br><br>Can be <b>misdiagnosed as bronchitis or pneumonia</b>.
What are the <b>S/S of HAPE</b>?	• <b>Shortness of breath</b>, <b>dry cough</b><br>• <b>Chest pain</b><br>• Cough may become productive — <b>frothy, reddish sputum</b><br>• <b>Low-grade fever</b>
What is the <b>Tx for HAPE</b>?	• <b>Descend ASAP</b><br>• <b>Supplemental oxygen</b><br>• <b>Gamow bag</b> or <b>hyperbaric chamber</b> for serious evacuation cases<br>• <b>Diamox</b> — medication that can be prescribed before high-elevation adventures
What is <b>HACE</b>, and what is the mechanism?	<b>High Altitude Cerebral Edema</b> — <b>fluid collects within the brain</b>, causing <b>increased intracranial pressure (ICP)</b>.
What are the <b>S/S of HACE</b>?	• <b>Headache</b> not relieved by rest or medication<br>• <b>Loss of coordination</b><br>• <b>Altered mental status</b>, <b>seizures</b>, <b>coma</b><br>• <b>Nausea</b>
What is the <b>Tx for HACE</b>?	• <b>Descend ASAP</b><br>• <b>Supplemental oxygen</b><br>• <b>Hydration</b><br>• <b>Gamow bag</b> or <b>hyperbaric chamber</b><br>• <b>Diamox</b>
What is the <b>critical evacuation rule</b> for a patient with HAPE or HACE?	<b>Immediately descend at least 1,000–1,500 ft</b> to relieve symptoms.<br><br>Follow with <b>complete evacuation to professional medical care</b>. <b>Go fast!</b>
What are the <b>prevention strategies</b> for altitude illness?	<b>Prevention is the best treatment:</b><br>• Plan a <b>staged ascent</b><br>• <b>Allow the body to adjust</b> before increasing physical demand<br>• <b>"Climb high, sleep low"</b><br>• Eat a <b>carbohydrate-rich diet</b> and <b>stay hydrated</b><br>• Consider carrying <b>Diamox</b> or other prescribed altitude meds
When assessing <b>skin signs</b>, what three things do you evaluate and what descriptors are used for each?	<b>CTM</b> — assessed as part of vitals:<br><br>• <b>C</b>olor — pink, pale, flushed, cyanotic (blue), jaundiced (yellow), mottled, ashen<br>• <b>T</b>emperature — hot, warm, cool, cold (use the back of your hand on the forehead or chest)<br>• <b>M</b>oisture — dry, moist, clammy, diaphoretic
What is the <b>normal baseline</b> for skin signs, and what's the classic <b>shock</b> presentation?	<b>Normal:</b> pink, warm, dry (<b>PWD</b>)<br><b>Shock:</b> pale, cool, clammy<br><br>A shift away from PWD is an early clue that perfusion or oxygenation is failing.
What are the NOLS <b>4 P's of Safety</b>, in priority order?	A priority-ordered safety check during <b>Scene Size-up</b>:<br><br><b>1. Personal</b> — your own safety first<br><b>2. Partner</b> — your rescue team<br><b>3. Public</b> — bystanders and other people on scene<br><b>4. Patient</b> — the patient last<br><br>Rationale: you can't help anyone if you become a second patient, so rescuers come before the patient.
Why are the NOLS 4 P's of Safety ordered <b>Personal → Partner → Public → Patient</b> rather than patient-first?	Because a rescuer who becomes a second patient doubles the problem and removes a helper. Prioritising your own safety, then your team's, then bystanders', preserves the resources needed to actually help the patient.
What does <b>PPE</b> stand for?	<b>Personal Protective Equipment.</b><br><br>Personal safety (the first of the 4 P's) begins with donning PPE before patient contact.
What does <b>BSI</b> stand for?	<b>Body Substance Isolation</b> — the principle of creating barriers between you and a patient's bodily fluids (blood, vomit, saliva, etc.) to prevent pathogen transmission.
What four items make up <b>PPE</b>, and when is each used?	• <b>Gloves</b> — always, for any patient contact<br>• <b>Face shield or particulate mask</b> — if respiratory-illness threats are present<br>• <b>Protective eyewear</b> — if spurting blood, vomit, or coughing is likely<br>• <b>Protective gown</b> — for large-area BSI from any bodily fluids
What can you use for hand protection if you don't have nitrile gloves?	Improvise a barrier: ski gloves, leather gloves, plastic bags, or any piece of plastic. Any barrier is better than bare hands.
What key principle about contagion should you assume with every patient?	<b>Assume everyone is contagious.</b><br><br>Anyone — regardless of age, race, gender, or demographic — can carry airborne or blood-borne pathogens <i>without showing any signs or symptoms</i>.
Which three <b>blood-borne pathogens</b> are of greatest concern in WFR?	<b>Hepatitis B</b>, <b>Hepatitis C</b>, and <b>HIV</b>.
When sizing up the scene, what four questions should you ask to <b>assess the numbers</b>?	• How many <b>patients vs. trained responders</b>?<br>• How many <b>helpful hands</b> are available for an evacuation?<br>• What <b>resources</b> do you have to aid evacuation?<br>• How far are you from the <b>trailhead or additional resources</b>?<br><br>Shapes your plan, pace, and evacuation decisions.
What is <b>triage</b>?	The process of categorising <b>patient priority</b> when you have <b>many patients</b> (more casualties than responders can treat at once).
How do you determine <b>patient priority</b> during triage?	By assessing <b>high-risk vs. low-risk</b> injury or illness. High-risk patients are treated and evacuated first.
